# BDR Outreach Composer style guide
Version: v1.0

This is an EXAMPLE style reference for BDR Outreach Composer writing. It is intentionally generic and product-agnostic. Use it with the positioning guide: the positioning guide chooses the buyer-safe frame; this guide explains how to make the email sound specific, credible, concise, and human.

Illustrative example — customize the phrase lists and examples below for your own product and voice. Treat every example as a pattern, not a copy-paste template. Strip greetings, signatures, unsafe internal signals, long sales sections, and anything that would not fit a first-touch body under the Outreach Composer word limit.

## Core principles

### Preserve judgment
The composer should reason from the research, not replace the writing step with deterministic templates. Use these patterns to improve judgment:
- choose the strongest public signal;
- cite the source type when it adds credibility;
- connect that signal to one practical product reason;
- write the note as a short human BDR email body.

### Stay safe and source-grounded
- Use only verifiable public facts or buyer-safe product familiarity.
- Do not imply private knowledge of roadmap, priorities, pain, budgets, usage, or internal evaluation.
- Prefer "I saw", "I noticed", "I heard", or "It looks like" when attached to a specific public source.
- If research is weak, mark that in the brief or evaluation. Do not say it in the buyer-facing email.
- Never expose internal labels such as exact usage, activity, credits, paid status, or internal counts.

### Keep the template boundary
Final email bodies must not include:
- greeting line;
- sign-off line;
- sender name;
- more than one soft question;
- em dashes;
- long product tours;
- feature lists.

The email template supplies greeting, signature, and sender context. The body should usually be 2 to 4 short paragraphs and stay under the runtime word limit.

### Prefer specificity over safe abstraction
The common failure mode is taking a strong research signal and flattening it into generic language. Do not turn a conference talk, launch post, repo, or executive quote into "I saw you are doing [category]."

Weak connector language to avoid (customize this list for your product):
- "related problem";
- "similar layer";
- "related area";
- "infrastructure side";
- "run, review, and control" with no specific surface or mechanism;
- "[add category filler phrases to avoid here]".

Better copy keeps the specific source and names one concrete reason your product is relevant.

## Source-credibility opener guidance
When research comes from a concrete public source, cite where or how you heard it instead of flattening it into a company-level observation.

Use this when it is natural:
- Talk or conference session: "I heard [person] talk at [event] about [specific idea]."
- Podcast or webinar: "I caught [person] on [podcast/webinar] discussing [specific point]."
- Launch post: "I saw the [product/feature] launch post, especially the part about [specific workflow]."
- Blog or engineering post: "Your post on [topic] stood out because [specific idea]."
- Repo or open-source project: "I saw the [repo/project] work around [specific technical surface]."
- Executive quote: "I saw [person]'s comment about [specific priority]."
- Docs or changelog: "I noticed the recent docs/update around [specific capability]."

Do not over-summarize their own work back to them. One credible reference is enough.

Weak: "I saw [Example Company] is working on [category]."
Better: "I saw [Example Company]'s launch post around [specific workflow]."

### Inbound campaign signal openers
When the lead interacted with your marketing content (ad click, PDF download, campaign email open), that signal is timing context, not source-credible evidence. It belongs to the same safety tier as product familiarity.

If the specific action can be named naturally, it can anchor the first line:
- "Saw you downloaded the [asset] and wanted to follow up."
- "I noticed you signed up for the [webinar], so wanted to share a quick note."

Do not use vague references to inbound activity that sound like surveillance or add no value:
- Bad: "It looks like [your product] recently came across your path."
- Bad: "In case you recently came across our materials, I wanted to follow up."

When the specific inbound action cannot be named naturally, skip it in the opener, use a public-evidence opener if one exists, and treat the inbound signal as quiet timing context.

## Opener specificity ladder
Use the highest rung the research safely supports.

### Best: named person plus exact venue/source plus specific idea
Pattern: "[Person]'s [talk/post/podcast/session] on [specific idea] stood out."
Why it works: it proves the note came from real research, avoids vague category language, and gives the product bridge a clear reason to exist.

### Good: exact company initiative plus specific product or research signal
Pattern: "I saw [Company]'s recent [launch/post/repo/hiring focus] around [specific workflow or technical surface]."

### Acceptable: company-level signal
Pattern: "I noticed [Company] has been publishing about [specific enough area]."
Keep the claim narrow, do not pretend the source proves priorities or pain, and pair it with a simple, concrete product bridge.

### Weak: broad category language
Avoid openers like "I saw you are doing [category]" or "Looks like [category] is a focus." These are only acceptable if no stronger research exists and the brief marks the direction as low confidence.

## Product bridge principle
The bridge should connect the source signal to one practical product reason. It should not be a positioning paragraph or a feature dump.

Use one concrete mechanism type from your product when the positioning guide supports it. Keep a customized list of your product's mechanisms here (for example: [mechanism 1], [mechanism 2], [mechanism 3]).

Rules:
- Pick one practical mechanism per draft unless the source specifically supports more.
- Make the product concrete with at least one mechanism when writing about work at team scale.
- If the bridge could be said by any competitor, make it more specific or choose a different frame.

Weak: "[Your product] is working on a related problem around the infrastructure side of the work."
Better: "[Your product] focuses on the part that comes after the first experiments: [one concrete mechanism, customized for your product]." (Illustrative example — customize.)

## Historical BDR patterns to preserve
Stronger past BDR notes were concrete. Preserve these traits:
- name the specific source signal before the product bridge;
- use concrete product vocabulary when evidence supports it;
- keep the tone casual and low-hype;
- write one useful reason to compare notes, not a mini product page;
- avoid telling the buyer what their own company is doing in detail.

Only generalize a pattern when the new lead has the same evidence type, similar buyer role, and same buyer-owned problem.

## Review feedback patterns to encode
Recurring review feedback tends to point to these fixes (customize as you learn):
- cite the specific source when available;
- avoid generic connector phrases like "related area", "similar layer", and "infrastructure side";
- make the product concrete with at least one mechanism;
- sound like a human BDR note, not a positioning paragraph;
- use public evidence before quiet product familiarity whenever possible.

## Good and weak examples
Keep a small set of generic contrasts here and replace them with real, customized product examples over time.

### Source credibility
Weak: "I saw you are working on [category]."
Better: "I saw your launch post on [specific workflow]."

### Concrete product bridge
Weak: "[Your product] helps with the infrastructure side of the work."
Better: "[Your product] helps teams [specific action] with [specific mechanism], so useful work is not stuck on one person's machine." (Illustrative example — customize.)

### One mechanism, not a feature dump
Weak: "[Your product] connects [integration 1], [integration 2], [integration 3], memory, evals, and permissions."
Better: "For teams turning experiments into recurring work, [your product] can [one concrete mechanism] while keeping the work visible." (Illustrative example — customize.)

### Human tone
Weak: "[Your product] is the [category] control plane that operationalizes autonomous workflows for enterprise teams."
Better: "The part [your product] focuses on is what happens after the work becomes a real team workflow: making it easier to [specific action]." (Illustrative example — customize.)

### Product familiarity
Weak: "We saw your team has paid usage and recent feature activity."
Better: "In case [your product] is already on your radar, I thought the [relevant work] could be relevant."

## Anti-patterns
Do not write (customize this list for your product):
- "[Your product] is in a related area."
- "This feels like a similar layer."
- "We help with the infrastructure side."
- "I saw you are doing [category]."
- "We saw your users using [your product]."
- "I did not find a strong public signal."

Replace with a source-specific opener, one buyer-owned problem, one practical product mechanism, and a soft, low-pressure reason to compare notes.

## Quick self-check before finalizing
Before passing drafts to deterministic validation, ask:
1. Is the opener on the highest safe rung of the specificity ladder?
2. Does it cite the source type when that makes the evidence more credible?
3. Is there one concrete product mechanism, not a broad category bridge?
4. Could the sentence only be said by your product, or could any competitor say it?
5. Does the body avoid unsafe internal signals, greetings, signatures, and salesy sections?
6. Is the note short enough to sound like a first BDR email?
7. Would a BDR be comfortable sending it with only light edits?

If the answer to any of the first four questions is no, rewrite for specificity before relying on structural validation.
