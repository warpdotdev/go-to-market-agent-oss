## Core rule: never stop at category language
The concreteness principle: category words are allowed only when paired with a mechanism the buyer can picture.

If a draft leans on abstract category language (for example "AI workflows," "team processes," or "control plane"), add one concrete mechanism unless doing so would create a feature dump.

Good mechanisms are specific and buyer-visible. Keep a short, customized list of the mechanisms your product actually offers, such as:
- [concrete mechanism 1 — customize for your product]
- [concrete mechanism 2 — customize for your product]
- [concrete mechanism 3 — customize for your product]

Bad: "[Your product] helps with the infrastructure side of the work."
Better: "[Your product] lets teams [do a specific action] and [review or trigger it a specific way]." (Illustrative example — customize for your product.)

## Frame selection rules
1. Pick the strongest buyer-relevant evidence, not the first fact in the research.
2. Choose one primary frame per email. A secondary clause is fine, but do not tour every product surface in one body.
3. Prefer public evidence over internal product activity when the public evidence is strong enough.
4. Use product familiarity only as quiet timing context. Do not reveal exact usage, paid status, or individual behavior.
5. Match the frame to what the person likely owns. An individual contributor needs a concrete workflow surface; a leader can absorb scale or governance language.
6. If the evidence is thin, mark the brief low-confidence or block for more research. Do not tell the prospect the research was weak.
7. Use one concrete product mechanism per body unless the source specifically supports more.
8. Sound like a human BDR note: one observation, one practical product connection, one simple reason to talk.

Buyer problem: early experiments become real team workflows, and teams then need ways to route, reuse, and control the work.

### `workflow_orchestration`
Use when evidence points to distributed teams, automation, or workflows that should run beyond one person's machine.

Buyer problem: useful work needs to run reliably, start from the right trigger, keep its context, and be visible enough for a team to trust.

Concrete mechanisms to pair with this frame: [customize for your product].

Good bridge: "[Your product] lets teams run [the work] from [a specific trigger] and then inspect it, instead of treating the run as a black box." (Illustrative example — customize.)

Avoid listing every integration in one sentence.

### `governance`
Use for leadership, platform ownership, security, compliance, cost discipline, or scaling adoption across teams.

Buyer problem: as usage spreads, teams need visibility, permissions, and auditability without slowing people down.

Concrete mechanisms to pair with this frame: [customize for your product].

Good bridge: "[Your product] gives teams one place to configure how the work runs, what it can access, and how it gets reviewed." (Illustrative example — customize.)

Avoid saying the buyer has a governance problem unless the source explicitly says so.

## Final body checklist
Before ranking a draft first, verify:
- The opener cites a specific public source when available.
- The product bridge includes at least one concrete mechanism unless the body is intentionally minimal.
- The body avoids generic connector phrases like "related area," "similar layer," and "infrastructure side."
- The wording could not be said by any competitor without changing the product noun.
- The role mapping makes sense for the lead.
- The copy sounds like a human note, not a positioning paragraph.
- The body stays within the Outreach Composer constraints: no greeting, no sign-off, 85 words or fewer, light personalization, no unsupported inside knowledge, and at most one question mark.
